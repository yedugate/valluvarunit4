Section 1

Introduction

The nutritive value of foods refers to their content of essential nutrients‚Äîproteins, carbohydrates, fats, vitamins, and minerals. This value is influenced by multiple factors from production through preparation and consumption.

Section 2

1. Physiological Factors

  • Age, sex, growth stage: Requirements differ (children vs adults; pregnancy/lactation).
  • Genetics: Influences metabolism, absorption, and needs.
  • Health status: Infections, chronic diseases can alter absorption/utilisation.
  • Physical activity: Higher activity raises energy and micronutrient needs.
Section 3

2. Environmental & Agricultural Factors

  • Soil quality & fertilisers: Soil nutrient richness affects crop content; excessive nitrogen may reduce vitamin C in some crops.
  • Climate & geography: Determine crop types and nutrient density.
  • Harvest timing: Nutrient levels vary with maturity; peak ripeness often maximises vitamins.

Heating & Blanching

High heat and long duration degrade sensitive vitamins (e.g., vitamin C, folate). Quick blanching can reduce enzyme activity before freezing while limiting losses.

Storage Conditions

  • Light: degrades riboflavin; store dairy/fortified milks in opaque containers.
  • Oxygen: promotes oxidation of fats and some vitamins; seal containers.
  • Heat: accelerates nutrient loss; prefer cool, dark storage.

Preservation

  • Pasteurisation: mild vitamin loss; safety benefit outweighs small reductions.
  • Freezing: minimal losses when quickly blanched and kept cold.
  • Dehydration: concentrates nutrients by weight; some heat‚Äësensitive vitamins decrease.
  • Canning: heat‚Äëinduced losses but long shelf‚Äëlife; retention of minerals/fibre is good.
Section 5

4. Socioeconomic & Cultural Factors

  • Economic status: Affects access to diverse, nutrient‚Äërich foods.
  • Food habits, religion, culture: Shape dietary patterns and nutrient intake.
  • Availability & affordability: Influence seasonal variety and quality.
Section 6

5. Psychological & Lifestyle Factors

  • Mental health (stress, depression) can alter appetite and choices.
  • Eating behaviour & knowledge: Preferences, cooking skills, label literacy affect nutrition.
Section 7

6. Genetic & Pathological Factors

  • Genetic disorders (e.g., cystic fibrosis) can impair digestion/absorption.
  • Infections & chronic disease: Increase needs; may cause malabsorption and losses.

Section 9

Exam Focus Points

  • Define factors affecting nutritive value.
  • Explain how processing (milling, cooking, preservation) affects nutrients.
  • Describe physiological influences on nutrient needs.
  • Discuss environmental/agricultural impacts on food quality.
  • Illustrate effects of cooking and storage (light, oxygen, heat, water).
  • Recognise socioeconomic, cultural, and psychological influences.
