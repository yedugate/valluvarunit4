Section A

Nutrition & Healthy Eating

1What are the key principles of healthy eating?
  • Balance: all major food groups daily.
  • Variety: rotate grains (incl. millets), mix colours, vary proteins.
  • Proportion: ½ veg+fruit, ¼ whole grains/millets, ¼ protein; small healthy fats.
  • Quality: minimally processed, high‑fibre, low added sugar/sodium.
  • Portion & mindfulness; Hydration (water first); Food safety; Cultural fit & sustainability.
2How do the five food groups contribute to a balanced diet?
  • Grains (whole/millets): energy, fibre, B‑vitamins.
  • Vegetables: vitamins/minerals/antioxidants, fibre.
  • Fruits: vitamin C, potassium, phytonutrients.
  • Protein foods: amino acids; iron/zinc/B12/omega‑3 depending on source.
  • Dairy/fortified alternatives: protein, calcium, vitamin D.
Section B

Five Food Groups

3What are the five food groups and their nutritional functions?
  • Grains: carbs for energy; fibre; B‑vitamins; iron (millets).
  • Vegetables: micronutrients, fibre, phytochemicals.
  • Fruits: vitamin C, potassium, antioxidants.
  • Protein foods: growth/repair; iron, zinc, omega‑3.
  • Dairy/alternatives: quality protein, calcium, vitamin D/B12.
4How can you incorporate all five food groups into daily meals?
  • Breakfast: millet upma + curd + fruit; or egg + whole‑grain toast; or sprouted moong chilla + buttermilk.
  • Lunch: ½ veg, ¼ whole grains/millet roti, ¼ dal/chicken/fish; curd.
  • Snack: fruit + nuts; chana sundal; yogurt with flaxseed.
  • Dinner: veg‑rich khichdi (dal+millet) + sautéed greens + milk/fortified alt.
Section C

Balanced Diet

5What defines a balanced diet and why is it important?

A balanced diet supplies adequate energy and all essential nutrients in the right proportions across the day. Benefits include growth, immunity, hormone balance, cognition, digestive health, and prevention of chronic disease while maintaining a healthy body weight.

6How do macronutrient proportions affect health in a balanced diet?
  • Typical adult ranges: Carb 45–65%, Fat 20–35%, Protein 10–35% of energy.
  • Higher fibre/complex carbs → better glycaemic control/satiety.
  • Adequate protein (~0.8–1.2 g/kg; higher for athletes/older adults) preserves lean mass.
  • Healthy fats (MUFA/PUFA; limit trans/saturated) aid heart/brain and fat‑soluble vitamin absorption.
Section D

Food for Patients

7What dietary considerations are important for diabetic patients?
  • Carb quality/quantity: low‑GI, high fibre; portion control.
  • Balanced plates; include healthy fats (nuts/seeds).
  • Regular timing; protein with carbs to blunt spikes.
  • Limit sugars/refined carbs; monitor response; coordinate with meds & activity.
  • Heart health: MUFA/omega‑3; reduce sodium. Individualise with clinician/dietitian.
8How does diet help manage blood pressure?
  • DASH‑style: vegetables, fruits, pulses, nuts; low‑fat dairy; whole grains; lean proteins.
  • Sodium: ~1,500–2,300 mg/day; read labels.
  • Potassium, calcium, magnesium: from produce, pulses, dairy/greens.
  • Healthy fats; weight control; alcohol moderation; activity; hydration.
Section E

Food for Children

9What are the nutritional requirements of children for healthy growth?
  • Energy for growth/play; Protein for tissues/immunity.
  • Key micronutrients: iron, calcium, vitamin D, iodine, zinc, vitamin A, B12/folate.
  • Fibre & fluids; limit ultra‑processed snacks/sugary drinks/excess salt.
10How can balanced meals be planned for children?
  • Colourful plates (≥3 colours of veg/fruit).
  • Smart snacks: peanut chikki, fruit+nut mix, idli+sambar, curd+banana.
  • School tiffin: veg upma with sprouts; chapati roll with paneer/egg & salad; lemon rice + peanuts + fruit.
  • Picky eaters: involve them; frequent small portions; dips; fortify familiar foods (e.g., ragi dosa).
Section F

Food for Working Women

11What are the unique nutritional needs of working women?
  • Iron & B12 for fatigue/anemia prevention.
  • Calcium & vitamin D for bone health.
  • Protein ~20–30 g per meal for satiety/lean mass.
  • Omega‑3 & choline (fish, eggs, flax/chia).
  • Regular meals; magnesium‑rich foods; hydration; life‑stage tailoring.
12What dietary strategies support energy and health in working women?
  • Plan ahead (batch cook); healthy snacks (nuts, roasted chana, fruit, yogurt).
  • Desk kit: water bottle, herbal tea, high‑fibre low‑sugar bar, seeds mix.
  • Protein + fibre at each meal; moderate caffeine; mindful late‑dinner portions.
  • Movement breaks 2–3 min every 30–60 min.
Section G

Food for Hard‑Working Men

13How does physical labour influence nutritional needs?
  • Higher energy & carbohydrate needs; time carbs around shifts.
  • Protein ~1.0–1.6 g/kg/day for repair.
  • Replace electrolytes (sodium, potassium) with sweat losses.
  • Micronutrients: iron, B‑vitamins, magnesium.
14Which nutrients are critical for maintaining strength and endurance?
  • Carbohydrates (whole grains/millets, potatoes, fruits).
  • Protein & leucine (dal, dairy, eggs, fish, soy).
  • Creatine from meat/fish (or supplement when appropriate).
  • Omega‑3 (fish; flax/chia); Electrolytes & fluids.
Section H

Food for Sportspersons

15What role do carbohydrates, proteins, and fats play in sports nutrition?
  • Carbs: ~3–12 g/kg/day by training load for performance & recovery.
  • Protein: ~1.2–2.0 g/kg/day; 20–40 g post‑exercise; distribute over 3–5 feedings.
  • Fats: 20–35% energy; include MUFA/PUFA; avoid very high fat just before intense sessions.
16How should hydration be managed during athletic activity?
  • Before: ~5–10 ml/kg 2–4 h pre‑event (top‑up 2–3 ml/kg if needed).
  • During: ~0.4–0.8 L/h; for long/hot sessions 6–8% carb + ~300–600 mg Na/L.
  • After: replace 125–150% of losses (1 kg ≈ 1 L); include sodium to aid retention.
Section I

Fat‑Free & High‑Fibre Foods

17What constitutes a fat‑free food and what are its implications?
  • Label claim typically means < 0.5 g fat/serving (jurisdiction dependent).
  • May still be high in sugar/sodium; fat removal can reduce satiety/flavour.
  • Do not eliminate healthy fats: nuts, seeds, olive/mustard/groundnut oil, fatty fish.
18What are the health benefits of a high‑fibre diet?
  • Metabolic: better glycaemic control; cholesterol lowering (soluble fibre).
  • Digestive: regularity; microbiome (SCFAs).
  • Weight: satiety; lower energy density.
  • Targets: ~25–38 g/day; increase gradually & hydrate.
  • Sources: whole grains/millets, pulses, vegetables, fruits, nuts/seeds.
Section J

Factors Affecting Nutritive Value of Food

19How do cooking methods impact nutritive value?
  • Water‑soluble vitamins (C, B) lost with prolonged boiling; prefer steam/microwave/pressure‑cook with minimal water.
  • Avoid repeated deep‑frying; oxidation/trans fats.
  • Soaking/sprouting/fermentation: reduce phytates; improve B‑vitamins/digestibility.
  • Cut size, lids, and reusing cooking water (soups/dals) help retention; safe cooling/refrigeration for quality.
20What environmental factors influence nutrient content?
  • Soil & variety (incl. biofortification) drive micronutrient levels.
  • Climate/season affect phytonutrients; seasonal, field‑grown often higher.
  • Post‑harvest: time, temperature, bruising; storage vs light/oxygen/heat.
Section K

Product Development

21What are the key stages of food product development?
  1. Opportunity & concept (consumer insight, nutrition brief, claims).
  2. Screening & regulatory check (standards, categories, allergens, claims).
  3. Bench formulation (ingredients, target nutrition, cost‑in‑use).
  4. Prototype & sensory (triangle tests, hedonic, texture profiling).
  5. Shelf‑life & safety (microbial, water activity, oxidation, packaging fit).
  6. Process design & scale‑up (unit operations; HACCP).
  7. Packaging (barriers: OTR/MVTR; format; labelling).
  8. Costing & supply chain (specs, vendors, QA).
  9. Pilot launch & iteration (consumer feedback; stability tracking).
  10. Commercialisation & post‑launch QA (complaints, CAPA, improvement).
22How do water, protein, carbohydrates, and lipids affect product formulation?
  • Water: texture (crisp vs soft), aw (microbial risk), freezing point, glass transition; hydration times & processing.
  • Proteins: emulsification, gelling, foaming, water‑binding; structure (paneer, yogurt, nuggets).
  • Carbohydrates: sweetness (sugars), bulk/viscosity (starch/soluble fibre), gelatinisation/retrogradation, Maillard with proteins.
  • Lipids: mouthfeel, lubrication, flavour release, oxidation stability; crystals (tempering), emulsions/aeration.
Section L

Water, Proteins, Carbohydrates, Lipids

23Why is water vital in food product quality and nutrition?
  • Medium for reactions & heat transfer; defines texture (juicy vs dry).
  • Water activity controls microbes & reaction rates—lower aw extends shelf life.
  • Hydration of macromolecules: protein functionality, starch gelatinisation, fibre swelling.
  • Consumer perception: freshness/succulence; contributes to satiety.
24What are the functional roles of proteins, carbohydrates, and lipids in foods?
  • Proteins: gluten networks; gels (egg/soy/dairy); foams (whipped egg/cream); emulsifiers (casein/soy); Maillard browning.
  • Carbohydrates: energy; textures from starch gels→caramels; fibres for water‑holding/fat‑mimicry; prebiotic effects.
  • Lipids: energy density; carry fat‑soluble vitamins; aeration (baked goods); flavour carrier; creaminess; moisture barriers (coatings).
Section M

Emulsions

25What are emulsions and how do they affect food texture and stability?
  • Definition: dispersions of immiscible liquids (oil/water) with one phase as droplets in the other (O/W like milk, W/O like butter).
  • Emulsifiers: proteins, phospholipids (lecithin), mono/diglycerides reduce interfacial tension and protect droplets.
  • Processing: shear/homogenisation ↓ droplet size → smoother mouthfeel, opacity, improved kinetic stability.
  • Stability: aim for small, uniform droplets; correct HLB; adequate viscosity. Watch for creaming/sedimentation, flocculation, coalescence, Ostwald ripening.
  • Texture: controls thickness, creaminess, spreadability; vital in mayonnaise, dressings, dairy beverages, ice cream.

Adult Plate — General Guidance

  • ½ plate: vegetables & fruits (≥3 colours/day)
  • ¼ plate: whole grains/millets
  • ¼ plate: protein foods (pulses/eggs/fish/lean meats/soy)
  • Small add‑ons: nuts/seeds/healthy oils; dairy or fortified alternative
  • Hydration: water first; limit sugar‑sweetened beverages
