Section 1

Definition

An emulsion is a mixture of two or more immiscible liquids (usually oil and water) where one liquid is dispersed as tiny droplets within the other. Emulsions are colloidal systems in which both the dispersed phase and the continuous phase are liquids.

Formation

Formed by vigorous mixing or homogenisation to disperse one liquid into fine droplets within the other. Smaller droplets increase surface area and kinetic stability.

Section 5

Stability & Destabilisation

Emulsions are thermodynamically unstable but can be kinetically stabilised for long periods.

  • Creaming/Sedimentation: Droplets rise or settle due to density difference.
  • Flocculation: Droplets aggregate without merging.
  • Coalescence: Droplets merge ‚Üí phase separation.
  • Phase inversion: O/W ‚Üî W/O on composition/shear/temperature change.
Section 6

Role of Emulsifiers

  • Amphiphilic molecules with hydrophilic and hydrophobic parts.
  • Adsorb at the oil‚Äìwater interface ‚Üí reduce interfacial tension, form barriers (electrostatic/steric) against coalescence.
  • Common choices: egg yolk lecithin, milk proteins, mustard mucilage, synthetic surfactants.
Section 7

Applications in Food

Emulsions underpin products such as mayonnaise, cream, butter, salad dressings, and ice cream, influencing texture, mouthfeel, stability, and appearance.

Section 9

Exam Focus Points

  • Define emulsion and list its types.
  • Describe roles of dispersed phase, continuous phase, and emulsifiers.
  • Explain mechanisms of formation and stabilisation (droplet size, viscosity, interfacial tension).
  • Discuss causes and types of destabilisation (creaming, flocculation, coalescence, inversion).
  • List common food emulsions and their importance.
